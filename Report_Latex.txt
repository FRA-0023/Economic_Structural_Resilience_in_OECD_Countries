% --------- ABSTRACT (single column, boxed) ---------------
\begin{mdframed}[style=abstractbox]
{\color{accentblue}\small ABSTRACT}
Traditional resilience rankings usually aggregate heterogeneous economic indicators into single composite scores, often introducing arbitrary weighting systems and forced linear rankings. This project proposes an alternative framework based on Partial Order Theory (POSet) to analyse economic structural resilience across OECD countries. Instead of constructing a single resilience index, countries are represented as multidimensional structural configurations and compared through Pareto dominance relations. The framework explicitly models incomparability between economies characterized by different structural trade-offs. The methodology separates structural ordering variables from crisis validation variables. A POSet is constructed using five pre-shock structural indicators: debt capacity, employment strength, R\&D intensity, tertiary human capital and energy security. Two systemic shocks are used for validation: the Global Financial Crisis, with a 2007 structural baseline, and the COVID-19 crisis, with a 2019 structural baseline. The resulting structures show high incomparability, with an incomparability ratio of 0.68 in 2007 and 0.77 in 2019. Frontier countries also show stronger descriptive validation results: they recover faster on average after both shocks and outperform non-frontier countries on five out of six post-shock macroeconomic validation indicators in both periods. The results support the use of POSet as a transparent, non-compensatory framework for studying economic resilience, while avoiding the interpretation of resilience as a single scalar ranking.
\end{mdframed}

\section{Introduction}

This paper investigates whether pre-shock economic structures are associated with stronger resilience outcomes across OECD countries during systemic crises. The central question is not which country is ``best'', but whether some structural configurations place countries in a stronger position before a shock occurs.

The project uses Partial Order Theory (POSet) because economic resilience is multidimensional. A country can be strong in innovation and human capital but weaker in debt capacity or energy security, while another country may show the opposite pattern. Reducing these different configurations to a single composite score would hide these trade-offs and force comparisons that may not be meaningful.

For this reason, the analysis does not construct a universal Economic Resilience Index. Instead, countries are represented through five pre-shock structural variables: debt capacity, employment strength, R\&D intensity, tertiary human capital and energy security. These variables are selected because they describe relatively stable economic capacities rather than short-term crisis outcomes.

The POSet is constructed separately for two structural baselines: 2007, before the Global Financial Crisis, and 2019, before the COVID-19 shock. The resulting partial orders are then validated using post-shock macroeconomic outcomes, including GDP recovery, unemployment, inflation, debt and productivity. This separation between ordering variables and validation variables is essential: the POSet measures structural capacity before the shock, while validation checks whether stronger structural positions are associated with better outcomes after the shock.

A key contribution of the framework is that it treats incomparability as an informative result. If two countries cannot be ordered because each has strengths in different dimensions, the model reports this directly rather than hiding the difference inside a weighted average. The framework therefore provides a structural map of economic resilience configurations, identifying frontier countries, dominated countries and incomparable profiles without forcing a complete linear ranking.

% =========================================================
\section{Research Objective}
% =========================================================

The main research question is:

\begin{quote}
Which structural economic configurations are associated with stronger resilience during systemic crises?
\end{quote}

Answering this question requires addressing a prior issue that many resilience scorecards leave implicit. Composite indicators often reduce several dimensions to one weighted value and therefore assume that every country can be placed on a single line. This assumption is problematic for resilience analysis because countries may rely on different structural strengths. One economy may have lower debt and stronger energy security, while another may have stronger innovation capacity and human capital. A single score would make these cases appear directly comparable even when their structural profiles are different.

The research question therefore has two parts. First, to what extent are OECD economies structurally comparable to one another? Second, among the countries that are comparable, do structurally dominant profiles show stronger post-shock outcomes? Partial Order Theory is suited to this double question because it tests for dominance relations without assuming that all countries must be comparable \cite{Posets}. Incomparability is therefore not treated as a modelling error, but as evidence that different economic structures may represent different pathways to resilience.

This framing is useful for policy interpretation. If one country structurally dominates another, it becomes reasonable to ask whether some features of the stronger profile can inform reform priorities. If two countries are incomparable, the framework signals that policy transfer should be interpreted more carefully because each country may have strengths in different dimensions. The purpose is not to produce a league table, but to support a more transparent comparison of structural resilience profiles.

\label{Acquisition}
% =========================================================

Answering the research question requires a dataset that separates what an economy looks like structurally before a shock from how it performs during one. The project adopts a reproducible data acquisition pipeline implemented in Python. All datasets are collected and merged into a unified country-year panel indexed by country and year. The workflow integrates OECD Data Explorer \cite{OECD}, Eurostat \cite{Eurostat} and World Bank \cite{WorldBank} sources.

Most macroeconomic variables are retrieved from OECD SDMX endpoints. These include R\&D expenditure, tertiary education attainment, GDP growth, unemployment, productivity and inflation-related indicators. Public debt is handled through a source-priority rule: Eurostat is preferred where available, while the World Bank public-debt series is used as a fallback. This avoids keeping two overlapping public-debt values for the same country-year pair and creates a single canonical public-debt variable.

Energy import dependency is retrieved from the World Bank and retained as an external vulnerability measure. Since higher raw energy dependency indicates greater exposure, the variable is direction-aligned into an energy security score before entering the POSet.

The Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI) are collected and processed as contextual information, but they are not used as a final ordering variable. This is a deliberate methodological choice. WGI is already a composite governance dataset built from several dimensions and underlying sources. Since the project criticizes hidden aggregation and forced composite rankings, including WGI as a main ordering variable would partly reintroduce a pre-aggregated composite inside the POSet itself. Governance remains relevant, but it is treated as contextual or sensitivity material rather than as one of the five baseline ordering dimensions.

The final dataset is divided into two subsets. The first subset contains the structural ordering variables used to construct the POSet. The second subset contains validation variables used only for post-shock evaluation. This separation prevents outcome leakage: the variables used to judge crisis performance are not allowed to influence the structural ordering.

% =========================================================
\subsection{Strengths and Weaknesses of the Data}
\label{sec:data_strengths_weaknesses}

The main strength of the dataset is its institutional provenance. All variables are drawn from official OECD, Eurostat or World Bank sources. This improves reproducibility because each variable can be retrieved again through documented APIs or public bulk downloads. It also makes the definitions externally auditable rather than specific to this project.

A second strength is the clear separation between structural variables and validation variables. The ordering variables describe pre-shock capacities, while the validation variables measure post-shock outcomes. This design is important because it allows the project to test whether the structural POSet has explanatory value without building the outcome directly into the ordering.

The main limitation is coverage. Complete-case requirements reduce the sample to 25 countries for the 2007 baseline and 35 countries for the 2019 baseline. This is preferable to filling structural gaps with artificial values, but it means the final POSet represents only countries with sufficient data coverage. A second limitation is that some potentially relevant dimensions, such as governance quality, industrial structure or supply-chain exposure, are not included in the final baseline POSet. In particular, WGI is not used as an ordering variable because it is already a composite measure; however, it remains useful for interpretation and possible future extensions.

% =========================================================
\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

The preprocessing phase harmonizes heterogeneous international datasets into a common country-year structure. Country identifiers and years are standardized, duplicate country-year-variable observations are checked, and source-specific inconsistencies are resolved before constructing the analytical panel.

Missing data are handled conservatively. The final POSet uses complete-case samples for the selected baseline year and the five ordering variables. Countries with incomplete structural profiles are excluded from the relevant baseline rather than being filled through interpolation. This reduces sample size but avoids creating artificial structural profiles.

All final ordering variables are direction-aligned so that higher values always mean a more favourable condition. Public debt and unemployment are inverted into debt capacity and employment strength, while energy import dependency is inverted into energy security. R\&D intensity and tertiary education already have a positive interpretation. The direction-aligned variables are then scaled into comparable 0--100 scores and transformed into five ordinal levels for POSet construction.

The POSet is constructed using single-year structural snapshots: 2007 for the Global Financial Crisis baseline and 2019 for the COVID-19 baseline. This choice reflects the purpose of the analysis: the unit of interest is the structural configuration of an economy immediately before a shock, not its average trajectory over a long period. Medium-term volatility measures are computed only as contextual diagnostics and are not included in the final baseline POSet.

The full pipeline is implemented in Python, using modular notebooks for acquisition, preprocessing, POSet construction, sensitivity analysis, validation and visualization. The code is structured so that each stage produces intermediate diagnostic outputs and final report-ready tables. All code procedures are available in the project repository \cite{GitHubCode}.

\section{Partial Order Formulation and Structural Profiles}
\label{sec:poset_construction}

The POSet translates multidimensional country profiles into a structure that does not force comparability where none exists. Each country is represented by five direction-aligned structural variables: debt capacity, employment strength, R\&D intensity, tertiary human capital and energy security. A country is considered structurally dominant only when it is at least as strong as another country in every dimension and strictly stronger in at least one dimension.

This rule is non-compensatory. A country cannot compensate for weakness in debt capacity, employment strength or energy security simply by having a high value in R\&D or tertiary education. This is the key difference between the POSet and a composite index. The POSet keeps the separate dimensions visible and reports when two countries cannot be compared without introducing arbitrary weights.

\subsection{Temporal Scope of Structural Measurement}
\label{sec:temporal_scope}

Each ordering variable is measured at a single pre-shock year: 2007 for the Global Financial Crisis baseline and 2019 for the COVID-19 baseline. This design keeps the structural ordering temporally prior to the crisis outcomes used for validation. It also makes the interpretation simple: the framework asks whether the structure observed before a shock is associated with later resilience outcomes.

The validation windows are separate from the ordering years. For the 2007 baseline, validation uses post-shock macroeconomic behaviour from 2008--2012. For the 2019 baseline, validation uses the available post-COVID period from 2020--2023. This separation protects the analysis from outcome leakage.

A multi-year average of the ordering variables could be used in future work to smooth short-term anomalies. In this project, however, the single-year snapshots are retained because the selected variables are relatively slow-moving structural indicators and because the objective is to represent the pre-shock configuration as clearly as possible.

\subsection{Ordinal Profiling}
\label{sec:ordinal_profiling}

The continuous structural scores are converted into five ordinal levels within each shock-specific baseline sample. Level 1 represents the weakest group in a dimension and level 5 represents the strongest group. The five dimension levels are then combined into a structural profile code. For example, a profile such as $3$-$5$-$5$-$2$-$2$ describes a country that is relatively strong in some dimensions and weaker in others.

This profiling step reduces the influence of small numerical differences. A very small difference in public debt or unemployment should not necessarily imply a different structural type. By using ordinal levels, the POSet focuses on broader structural configurations rather than minor decimal-level variation.

\subsection{Dominance Rule and Epsilon-Margin Robustness}
\label{sec:epsilon_dominance}

The main profile POSet uses strict Pareto dominance on the ordinal profiles. A profile dominates another profile if it is at least as good in all five dimensions and better in at least one dimension. This produces the main Hasse diagrams and the main frontier results.

As a robustness check, the analysis also applies an $\epsilon$-margin rule on normalized country-level scores. Under this rule, dominance requires not only that one country is at least as good in every dimension, but also that it has a minimum advantage in at least one dimension. This avoids interpreting negligible numerical differences as meaningful structural superiority. The epsilon-margin analysis is a robustness check, not a replacement for the main profile-level POSet.

\subsection{Graph Reduction and Structural Incomparability}
\label{sec:graph_reduction}

The dominance relations between profiles form a directed acyclic graph. A transitive reduction is then applied to remove redundant indirect relations and produce a Hasse diagram. In the diagram, an edge represents a direct dominance relation between two profiles.

The absence of a path between two profiles is also meaningful. It means the profiles are structurally incomparable: each may have strengths in different dimensions, and neither can be said to dominate the other without introducing subjective trade-offs. This is one of the central outputs of the project. High incomparability shows why a single ranking would hide important structural information.

\subsection{Empirical POSet Results}
\label{sec:poset_results}

The empirical POSet was constructed separately for the 2007 and 2019 baselines. The final specification uses five ordering variables and five ordinal levels. The 2007 sample contains 25 countries and 25 distinct profiles. The 2019 sample contains 35 countries and 34 distinct profiles. In both years, the incomparability ratio is high, which confirms that OECD economies cannot be adequately described by a complete ranking.

% =========================================================
\section{Shock Validation}
\label{sec:shock_validation}
% =========================================================

The objective of the validation stage is to assess whether the pre-shock structural ordering contains meaningful information about resilience during systemic crises. The POSet is constructed only from pre-shock structural variables, while validation uses independent post-shock outcomes. This ensures that the validation procedure tests the explanatory value of the structural hierarchy rather than reproducing information already embedded in it.

\subsection{Validation Design}

The validation design follows a simple logic. If the selected structural variables capture resilience-related capacities, countries in stronger POSet positions should show more favourable outcomes after a systemic shock. The analysis evaluates this idea in two ways.

First, GDP recovery validation compares the average recovery time of frontier and non-frontier countries. Recovery time measures how long it takes an economy to return to its pre-shock GDP path. Second, multi-indicator validation compares frontier and non-frontier countries across six macroeconomic outcomes: average GDP growth, average unemployment, unemployment change, absolute inflation, public debt change and productivity change.

The validation windows are 2008--2012 for the Global Financial Crisis baseline and 2020--2023 for the COVID-19 baseline. These variables are not used to construct the POSet. They are used only after the structural profiles have been defined.

\subsection{GDP Recovery Validation}

The GDP recovery results support the structural interpretation of the POSet. In the 2007 baseline, frontier countries recover faster on average by approximately 0.82 years compared with non-frontier countries. In the 2019 baseline, frontier countries recover faster on average by approximately 0.26 years. These differences are descriptive and should not be interpreted causally, but they suggest that structurally undominated profiles are associated with stronger recovery outcomes.

The multi-indicator validation provides a broader test of the POSet structure. For both the 2007 and 2019 shocks, frontier countries outperform non-frontier countries on five out of six available macroeconomic validation indicators. In 2007, the only contradictory indicator is productivity change, which favours non-frontier countries. In 2019, the only contradictory indicator is unemployment change, which also favours non-frontier countries.

\section{Conclusion}
% =========================================================

This project examined whether pre-shock economic structures are associated with stronger resilience during major crises in OECD countries. Rather than creating one composite resilience ranking, the analysis used Partial Order Theory to compare countries across five structural dimensions: debt capacity, employment strength, R\&D intensity, tertiary human capital and energy security.

The main result is that OECD countries cannot be described adequately through a simple ranking from strongest to weakest. In both the 2007 and 2019 baselines, many country pairs are structurally incomparable. The incomparability ratio is 0.68 in 2007 and 0.77 in 2019. This means that countries often combine strengths and weaknesses in different ways: one country may have stronger innovation and human capital, while another may have stronger debt capacity or energy security. The POSet framework makes these trade-offs visible instead of hiding them inside a weighted average.

The analysis also identifies Pareto-frontier countries. These countries are not necessarily best in every dimension, but they are not structurally dominated by another observed profile. There are 8 frontier countries in the 2007 baseline and 13 in the 2019 baseline. The robustness analysis supports the stability of the dominance logic: all tested epsilon-margin values remain valid partial orders, with no reciprocal dominance cycles.

The validation stage provides supportive descriptive evidence. Frontier countries recover faster on average after both shocks: by approximately 0.82 years in the 2007 Global Financial Crisis baseline and 0.26 years in the 2019 COVID-19 baseline. They also outperform non-frontier countries on five out of six post-shock macroeconomic validation indicators in both periods. This suggests that the structural POSet captures meaningful aspects of economic resilience capacity.

These results should be interpreted carefully. The framework does not predict crises, and it does not prove that structural position alone causes a particular crisis outcome. Crisis performance is also influenced by policy responses, shock exposure, industrial structure, international conditions and country-specific circumstances. The value of the POSet framework is that it provides a transparent, non-compensatory way to compare resilience structures while preserving incomparability.

Future work could extend the framework by using multi-year averages for structural baselines, adding additional shock episodes and incorporating further dimensions such as supply-chain exposure, industrial structure or economic complexity. Governance indicators could also be analysed more deeply as a contextual or sensitivity dimension, but care is needed because governance datasets such as WGI are already composite indicators. The final contribution of this project is therefore not a universal Economic Resilience Index, but a structural framework for identifying frontier, dominated and incomparable resilience profiles.
