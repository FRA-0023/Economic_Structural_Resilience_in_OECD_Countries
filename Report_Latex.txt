\section{Introduction}

This paper investigates whether structural economic configurations,
characterized prior to a systemic shock, are associated with
differential resilience outcomes across OECD countries.
The central question is not which country performs best, but whether
structurally dominant economies, as identified through a multidimensional
partial ordering, demonstrate stronger adaptive capacity during crisis
conditions.

To answer this question, the paper constructs a Partially Ordered Set
(POSet) from five slow-moving structural indicators R\&D expenditure,
institutional quality, public debt, energy import dependency and tertiary
education attainment using data drawn from OECD \cite{OECDResilience}, Eurostat and World
Bank sources.
The resulting structural hierarchy is then validated against two
empirical shock episodes: the 2008 Global Financial Crisis and the
COVID-19 pandemic.

A key methodological commitment of the paper is to treat structural
incomparability as a substantive finding rather than a modelling failure.
Where no dominance relation exists between two economies, this is
reported and interpreted directly.
The framework is therefore an analytical tool
for identifying which structural configurations may support stronger
adaptation under systemic stress.
To answer this question, we need a framework that (a) does not presuppose comparability and (b) separates structural conditions from shock outcomes. Partial Order Theory satisfies both requirements.

\section{Research Objective}
% =========================================================

The main research question is:

\begin{quote}
Which structural economic configurations are associated with stronger resilience during systemic crises?
\end{quote}

Answering this question requires resolving a prior issue that most existing resilience frameworks leave unexamined. Composite resilience indices, such as the type of vulnerability scorecards published by the WEF, the IMF and the OECD itself, reduce multiple structural dimensions to a single weighted scalar and in doing so they implicitly assume that any two countries can always be meaningfully ranked against one another. This assumption is rarely tested; it is built into the aggregation step itself. The consequence is that two countries which achieve resilience through fundamentally different structural routes, for instance one relying on fiscal conservatism and energy dependency, the other on fiscal flexibility and energy independence, can be assigned similar or directly comparable scores, even though their underlying configurations are not actually comparable in any economically meaningful sense. The research question pursued in this project therefore has two parts. The first is the precondition: to what extent are OECD economies structurally comparable to one another at all, as opposed to occupying distinct, non-comparable configurations? The second, which can only be answered honestly once the first has been addressed, is the original question: among the economies that are structurally comparable, does occupying a dominant position predict stronger resilience during systemic crises?

Partial Order Theory is suited to this double question precisely because it does not presuppose comparability the way composite indices do. Rather than forcing a linear ranking onto every country pair, it tests directly for the existence of a dominance relation and treats incomparability not as a modelling failure but as a substantive and informative outcome in its own right \cite{Posets}.

This double question is relevant to two distinct categories of stakeholders, each engaging primarily with one half of it. Policymakers and international institutions responsible for designing structural reform programmes, such as the EU Commission or the OECD itself, are primarily concerned with the comparable cases: if two economies are found to stand in a clear dominance relation, this provides a methodologically grounded basis for asking whether a reform path that succeeded in the dominant economy could plausibly transfer to the dominated one. Central banks, by contrast, are equally interested in the incomparability findings on their own terms, independently of crisis outcomes: if two member economies are found to be structurally incomparable ahead of a shock, this is itself relevant to understanding why a single monetary policy stance may transmit asymmetrically across them.

\section{Data Acquisition & Preprocessing Pipeline}
\label{Acquisition}
% =========================================================

Answering the research question requires a dataset that separates what an economy looks like structurally before a shock from how it 
performs during one; this section describes how that dataset is built.
The project adopts a fully reproducible data acquisition pipeline implemented in Python. All datasets are collected and merged into a unified country-year panel structure indexed by country and year. The workflow integrates Eurostat \cite{Eurostat}, Worldwide Governance Indicators \cite{WGI} (WGI) and the OECD Data Explorer \cite{OECD}, with a source hierarchy chosen to avoid redundant downloads and duplicate country-year observations.

Most macroeconomic and structural variables are retrieved directly from the OECD API. These include R\&D expenditure, tertiary education attainment, GDP growth, unemployment, productivity and inflation-related indicators. Public debt is handled differently: the pipeline now relies primarily on Eurostat for the public-debt series and merges it with the World Bank fallback only where Eurostat coverage is unavailable. This design choice removes the earlier OECD public-debt download and avoids maintaining two overlapping public-debt series for the same countries, creating a single source of truth for the following processes.  

Institutional robustness is approximated using governance-related indicators from the Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI) database provided by the World Bank. The composite draws on all six available dimensions: Voice and Accountability, Political Stability and Absence of Violence, Government Effectiveness, Regulatory Quality, Rule of Law and Control of Corruption. Given that governance indicators evolve considerably more slowly than macroeconomic variables, they are treated as quasi-static structural variables within the ordering framework rather than as cyclical signals.
To aggregate the six WGI dimensions into a single institutional robustness score, a correlation-aware distance-to-ideal method is employed. Each country-year observation is evaluated against an ideal governance profile defined as the maximum attainable score across all six dimensions. The proximity of each observation to this ideal is then computed using the Mahalanobis distance, which accounts for the correlation structure among the dimensions and thereby reduces the double-counting that would otherwise arise from naively averaging indicators that are, in practice, substantially collinear. The resulting score can be interpreted as a composite measure of how close a country's institutional configuration is to the governance frontier, adjusted for the redundancy inherent in the underlying dimensions.

Because the project combines heterogeneous international sources, a harmonization procedure is required before any structural analysis can be performed. The preprocessing phase is kept deliberately simple: it aligns sources, removes duplicate country-year observations and makes each indicator comparable without changing its meaning. Variables characterized by negative structural interpretation, such as debt and energy dependency, are inverted after normalization so that larger values correspond to structurally preferable conditions. In this framework, energy dependency is treated on a 0--100 scale where 100 represents maximum independence.

The final dataset is divided into two distinct subsets. The first subset contains the ordering variables used to construct the POSet, while the second subset contains validation variables used exclusively for shock-based evaluation. This separation aims to distinguish structural economic configuration from post-shock economic performance.

The complete workflow is modular and organized into acquisition, preprocessing, POSet construction and validation stages to ensure reproducibility and future extensibility of the framework. In practice, each stage has a narrowly defined role: acquisition gathers source data, preprocessing resolves duplicates and aligns directionality, POSet construction performs the structural ordering and validation evaluates whether the ordering has explanatory power under shock conditions.

\subsection{Strengths and Weaknesses of the Data}
\label{sec:data_strengths_weaknesses}

The dataset presents a specific profile of strengths and weaknesses that must be evaluated directly against the goal of constructing a structural ordering of resilience, rather than assessed in general terms.

The principal strength of the data lies in its provenance. Every source employed, OECD SDMX, Eurostat and the World Bank, including the Worldwide Governance Indicators, is an official institutional source rather than a scraped, proprietary, or third-party aggregated dataset. This has two direct consequences for the goal of the study. First, it ensures that the pipeline is fully reproducible, since every variable can be retrieved again through a documented public API or bulk download, which is consistent with the project's stated emphasis on a transparent and reproducible workflow. Second, it means that any structural ordering derived from the data rests on measurement choices that are externally documented and audited by the issuing institutions themselves, rather than on definitions internal to this project.

A limitation concerns the construction of the institutional robustness variable itself. The Worldwide Governance Indicators are not a primary measurement in the way that, for instance, public debt or R\&D expenditure are; they are themselves a composite built from underlying expert assessments and perception-based surveys. This means that one of the five structural variables used to construct the POSet, the variable intended to most directly represent the quality of institutions, already embeds an aggregation methodology of the kind that this project otherwise sets out to avoid at the level of the overall resilience ranking. While the correlation-aware Mahalanobis aggregation described in Section~\ref{sec:poset_construction} mitigates double-counting across the six WGI dimensions, it does not resolve this underlying dependence on a composite source and this should be acknowledged as a constraint inherited from the available data rather than a limitation introduced by the project's own methodology.

The directional vectors ($\uparrow$ and $\downarrow$) dictate the partial ordering logic. Research and Development (R\&D) expenditure and tertiary education track the technological and human capital frontier, determining an economy's capacity to reallocate resources dynamically during a crisis. The Worldwide Governance Indicators provide a composite measure of institutional quality, rule of law and regulatory control, which directly influence policy execution speed under stress. Conversely, high ratios of public debt and energy import dependency operate as severe structural constraints. Public debt restricts fiscal maneuvering space when counter-cyclical spending is required, while energy dependency exposes the domestic production function to external supply-side geopolitical shocks. For this reason, the public-debt series is treated as a priority merge of authoritative sources rather than as an independently re-estimated variable.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
\label{sec:preprocessing}

The preprocessing phase plays a central methodological role due to the heterogeneous and partially incomplete nature of international macroeconomic datasets. Before constructing the POSet, all data streams must be harmonized to ensure structural comparability across nations and observation years, but only to the extent required to preserve the original semantics of each indicator.

To consolidate disparate data sources into a unified analytical environment, the methodology relies on a strict relational integration procedure. The various international datasets are systematically merged using unique Country Identifiers (IDs) and observation years as primary keys. This ensures precise cross-sectional and temporal alignment, preventing mismatched economic data from distorting a country's structural profile.

In handling incomplete data, the framework prioritizes empirical integrity over sample size. Rather than synthesizing missing values through temporal interpolation—which risks introducing artificial continuity and masking genuine structural gaps, the methodology enforces strict exclusion criteria. Any country exhibiting a high density of missing observations across the required structural variables is removed entirely from the analysis. By eliminating incomplete profiles rather than statistically approximating them, the framework prevents artificial data from corrupting the partial order. This filtering is a deliberate design choice: the aim is not to maximize the number of observations at any cost, but to preserve a reliable structural comparison.

Duplicate public-debt observations are resolved by source priority, with Eurostat preferred over World Bank when both are available, so that the same country-year pair is not counted twice.

Finally, continuous variables are subjected to normalization to neutralize unit variances across highly diverse metrics. To accommodate the heavy-tailed distributions and extreme outliers common in international macroeconomic data, robust scaling techniques (such as those based on the median and interquartile range) are prioritized over classical z-score standardization, ensuring that the structural distances between the countries remain stable and interpretable rather than distorted by scale.


A methodological note is required on the temporal scope of the structural measurement.
The POSet is constructed using single-year snapshots of the ordering  variables, taken at 2007 and 2019 respectively for the two shock baselines. The unit of analysis is the structural configuration of an economy at 
a specific pre-shock moment, not its average trajectory over a longer window.
The implicit assumption is that slow-moving structural variables like R\&D expenditure, institutional quality, public debt, energy import dependency and tertiary education are sufficiently stable year-to-year that a single observation reliably represents the 
underlying structural condition rather than a transient fluctuation.
This assumption is partially supported by the epsilon-margin robustness analysis in Section~\ref{sec:poset_robustness}, which 
demonstrates that the Pareto frontier membership remains stable across a range of dominance thresholds, suggesting that the 
structural conclusions are not driven by marginal numerical differences in the baseline year.
Nonetheless, the use of a single-year snapshot remains a limitation of the current framework: a multi-year pre-shock average would  provide a more robust structural baseline, and this represents a 
natural direction for future refinement.

\section{Partial Order Formulation and Structural Profiles}
\label{sec:poset_construction}

With the structural and validation variables defined, the next step is to translate multi-dimensional country profiles into a hierarchy 
that does not force comparability where none genuinely exists. The construction of the Partially Ordered Set (POSet) \cite{Posets} begins with a deliberate selection of socio-economic dimensions. Rather than evaluating cyclical performance, the structural hierarchy relies exclusively on five slow-moving macroeconomic variables: Research and Development expenditure, Worldwide Governance Indicators, Public debt, Energy import dependency and Tertiary education. The logical premise is that these specific indicators establish the underlying capacity of a socio-economic system to absorb and adapt to external stress long before a crisis actually occurs.

To construct a coherent mathematical space, these dimensions must share a uniform directional logic. A continuous direction-alignment process is applied to guarantee that higher values systematically map onto greater socio-economic advantages. All five vectors are normalized into a continuous $[0, 1]$ interval via Min-Max scaling. This strictly bounds the empirical frontier of the sample to unity and the baseline limit to zero, neutralizing unit variances and allowing percentages to be evaluated alongside institutional indices.

\subsection{Temporal Scope of Structural Measurement}
\label{sec:temporal_scope}

Each of the five ordering variables is measured at a single pre-shock
year: 2007 for the Global Financial Crisis baseline and 2019 for the
COVID-19 baseline.
This choice is justified by the nature of the variables themselves:
R\&D expenditure, governance quality, public debt, energy import
dependency and tertiary education attainment are slow-moving structural
indicators by design, selected precisely because they do not change
materially from year to year under normal macroeconomic conditions.
A single pre-shock observation is therefore treated as a reliable
proxy for the underlying structural configuration rather than a
transient fluctuation.
This temporal scope applies exclusively to the ordering variables used
to construct the POSet; validation variables employ multi-year
post-shock windows (2008--2010 for the GFC and 2020--2022 for
COVID-19), ensuring a clean separation between the structural baseline
and the crisis performance being evaluated.
The one exception within the structural variable set is the optional
GDP-growth volatility indicator, which uses longer pre-shock windows
(2000--2007 and 2012--2019 respectively) to capture medium-term
macroeconomic stability; however, this variable is not included in the
final baseline POSet specification reported here.
A multi-year average of the ordering variables would provide a more
robust structural baseline by smoothing any transient anomaly in the
snapshot year, and this remains a natural direction for future
refinement of the framework.

\subsection{Ordinal Profiling and the Eradication of Noise}
\label{sec:ordinal_profiling}

While continuous metrics track granular marginal variances, they introduce measurement noise. In socio-economic reality, a fractional decimal-level difference in public debt does not constitute a fundamentally different economic paradigm. To bridge the gap between continuous quantitative variations and qualitative structural categories, the methodology introduces a discrete profiling mechanism. 

For each individual dimension, the continuous data is stratified into five distinct ordinal levels, corresponding to five equal quantile divisions of the unified dataset. A status score of zero designates the weakest structural tier within that specific dimension, while a score of four marks the absolute performance frontier. By stacking these discrete scores sequentially across all five evaluation vectors, every country is assigned a unified, multi-character alphanumeric string, which functions as its unique macroeconomic profile. The logical value of this step is profound: it condenses economies with highly similar macroeconomic configurations into shared structural states. By eradicating minor analytical noise, the framework ensures that the downstream hierarchy evaluates persistent macroeconomic realities rather than stochastic data fluctuations.

\subsection{The strict $\epsilon$-Margin Dominance Protocol}
\label{sec:epsilon_dominance}

The central challenge in this type of problems is determining how to rank complex entities without imposing arbitrary weights or allowing severe flaws in one area to be masked by extreme performance in another. To resolve this, the framework rejects compensatory utility models and deploys a strict $\epsilon$-margin dominance rule over the generated profile vectors.

Unlike epsilon-tolerant models that might allow a profile to slightly underperform in certain dimensions while compensating elsewhere, this approach utilizes epsilon exclusively as a minimum advantage threshold to prevent cyclic dominance. Formally, a macroeconomic profile strictly dominates another if and only if two rigid conditions are met. 
First, the dominant profile must be at least as good as the subordinate profile across every single scaled variable; it is not permitted to be worse in any dimension. Second, the dominant profile must exceed the subordinate profile by a margin strictly greater than the predefined epsilon threshold in at least one distinct variable. 
This implements a severe but logically consistent socio-economic value system: true structural superiority requires absolute baseline parity across all institutional and economic fields, coupled with a definitive, quantifiable advantage in at least one critical sector.

\subsection{Graph Reduction and Structural Incomparability}
\label{sec:graph_reduction}

The application of the dominance protocol yields a matrix of cross-country relations. To extract a comprehensible hierarchy, these unique structural states are mapped as nodes within a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). The framework applies a formal transitive reduction, an algorithmic operation that eliminates all redundant, indirect paths of dominance. The resulting Hasse diagram visualizes only the direct, essential structural relationships.

Within this graphical layout, a downward directed path between nodes indicates comparability, a definitive socio-economic dominance of one structural state over another. However, the most critical socio-economic insights often arise from the absence of a path. When two profiles lack a directed connection, they are deemed structurally incomparable. This incomparability is not a failure of the model, but a reflection of a core socio-economic reality: nations can and do evolve divergent, highly specialized, yet equally effective institutional models. By mapping incomparability rather than forcing an artificial ranking, the POSet acknowledges parallel development paths that cannot be objectively weighed against each other without resorting to subjective bias.



\subsection{Empirical POSet Results}
\label{sec:poset_results}

The empirical POSet was constructed separately for the two shock baselines: 2007 for the Global Financial Crisis and 2019 for the COVID-19 crisis. The final specification uses the baseline structural variable set and a discrete four-level profile representation for visualization, while five-level profiles are retained as the main analytical configuration in the robustness notebooks.

For the 2007 baseline, the four-level POSet contains 25 countries and 25 distinct structural profiles. The resulting Hasse diagram contains 51 direct edges after transitive reduction, with a comparability ratio of 0.2767 and an incomparability ratio of 0.7233. This indicates that most country pairs cannot be linearly ordered without imposing additional subjective weights. Eight countries are located on the Pareto frontier, corresponding to 32.00\% of the sample.

For the 2019 baseline, the four-level POSet contains 32 countries and 31 structural profiles. The Hasse diagram contains 62 direct edges, with a comparability ratio of 0.2710 and an incomparability ratio of 0.7290. Thirteen countries are classified as Pareto-frontier countries, representing 40.62\% of the sample. The persistence of high incomparability in both shock baselines confirms the central premise of the project: OECD economies exhibit structurally heterogeneous resilience configurations that cannot be reduced to a single linear ranking without losing important information.

\subsection{Robustness of the Partial Order}
\label{sec:poset_robustness}

To verify that the POSet structure was not an artefact of a single arbitrary dominance threshold, the analysis was repeated using an $\epsilon$-margin dominance rule. Under this rule, a country is allowed to dominate another only if it is at least as good across all dimensions and exceeds it by a minimum margin in at least one dimension. This avoids the risk of treating negligible numerical differences as meaningful structural superiority.

Across the tested margin values, the $\epsilon$-margin specification remained a valid partial order, with no reciprocal dominance cycles. In the 2007 baseline, the Pareto frontier remained stable at 13 countries across margin values from 0.00 to 0.20, with a Jaccard stability score of 1.00 relative to the zero-margin baseline. In the 2019 baseline, the Pareto frontier also remained stable at 21 countries across the same margin range, again producing a Jaccard stability score of 1.00.

\section{Shock Validation}
\label{sec:shock_validation}

The objective of the validation stage is to assess whether the structural ordering produced by the POSet contains meaningful information regarding economic resilience during systemic crises. While the POSet is constructed exclusively from pre-shock structural variables, resilience is evaluated using independent post-shock outcomes as explained in the sections before. This separation ensures that the validation procedure tests the explanatory value of the structural hierarchy rather than reproducing information already embedded in the ordering process.

\subsection{Validation Design}

The validation framework follows a simple causal logic. If the structural variables selected for the POSet genuinely capture resilience-related characteristics, countries occupying stronger structural positions should exhibit more favorable economic outcomes during subsequent crises.

To test this proposition, the ordering variables and validation variables remain strictly separated throughout the analysis. The POSet is constructed exclusively from the five structural indicators described in Section~\ref{Acquisition}, while crisis performance is evaluated using an independent set of outcome variables.

For the Global Financial Crisis, the structural baseline corresponds to 2007, while resilience outcomes are measured during the post-shock period. For the COVID-19 episode, the structural baseline corresponds to 2019 and resilience outcomes are evaluated over the subsequent pandemic years.

This design allows the structural ordering to function as an ex-ante representation of economic resilience capacity rather than as a contemporaneous description of crisis performance.

\subsection{Non-Parametric Statistical Framework}
\label{sec:stat_validation}

The validation methodology utilizes non-parametric statistical procedures to accommodate the ordinal nature of the POSet levels. Since the hierarchy does not assume an interval scale between tiers, traditional parametric tests are replaced by rank-based assessments.

The Spearman Rank Correlation coefficient is employed to measure the monotonic association between a country’s structural level and its aggregated resilience score. Validation is confirmed by a statistically significant negative correlation, indicating that structural weakness (higher level) corresponds to diminished crisis performance. Complementarily, the Kruskal-Wallis test is applied to determine if the resilience distributions across the various POSet tiers are statistically distinct, ensuring that the observed performance gaps are driven by structural positioning rather than stochastic variance.

\section{Conclusion}
% =========================================================

This project developed a POSet-based framework for comparing economic structural resilience across OECD countries without reducing heterogeneous economic indicators into a single weighted composite index. By using Partial Order Theory, the analysis preserves the multidimensional nature of resilience and explicitly represents structural incomparability between countries.

The empirical POSet results demonstrate that OECD economies cannot be adequately represented through a simple linear ranking. Instead, countries form distinct structural profiles based on different combinations of innovation capacity, institutional robustness, fiscal space, energy independence and human capital. The high incomparability ratios observed in both the 2007 and 2019 baseline structures confirm that resilience can emerge through alternative structural configurations rather than through one universally dominant development model.

The robustness analysis further supports this conclusion. The epsilon-margin procedure produced no reciprocal dominance cycles and showed stable Pareto-frontier membership across the tested margin range. This indicates that the main structural findings are not driven by negligible numerical differences or by an arbitrary dominance threshold.

The shock validation stage evaluates whether these ex-ante structural positions are associated with observed macroeconomic resilience during the Global Financial Crisis and the COVID-19 crisis. The final results indicate [INSERT FINAL VALIDATION INTERPRETATION: whether structurally stronger POSet positions were associated with better resilience outcomes]. Therefore, the POSet framework should be interpreted not as a direct crisis-prediction model, but as a transparent analytical tool for identifying structural configurations that may support stronger adaptation under systemic stress.

Overall, the project contributes a non-compensatory and interpretable framework for economic resilience analysis. It avoids arbitrary weighting schemes, avoids forced linear rankings, separates structural conditions from crisis outcomes and recognizes that countries may follow multiple structurally valid paths toward resilience.

Future developments may include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item dynamic temporal POSet evolution,
    \item multilayer dominance structures,
    \item network-based economic dependency analysis,
    \item integration with economic complexity indicators,
    \item extension to additional shock episodes and regional country samples.
\end{itemize}
